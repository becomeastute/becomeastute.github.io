Executive Summary

Bottom Line: Hip pain in athletes is complex—FAI, labral tears, groin pain, and hip dysplasia often overlap. FAI surgery allows 74% of athletes to return to preinjury competition level (67-81% CI), though 12.1% fail to return (mostly due to persistent hip pain). Conservative management effective for many: 65% pain reduction with active rehab for groin pain, 55% of labral tear athletes return to sport conservatively vs 79% surgically (but surgery means 324 days lost vs 27 days). Hip dysplasia: PAO (periacetabular osteotomy) allows greater than 70% return to sport in competitive athletes, even high-impact sports. Key: accurate diagnosis critical—cam morphology present in 37-55% of asymptomatic athletes, labral tears in 33-54% of pain-free hips. Imaging findings don't equal pathology.1-21

2020-2025 Meta-Analyses: FAI surgery: 74% return to preinjury level (1,634 athletes) • 12.1% fail to return (74.3% hip-related reasons, mostly persistent pain) • Conservative management: 65% pain reduction with active rehab at 6 weeks • Labral tears: 33-54% prevalence in asymptomatic athletes • Groin pain: rehabilitation faster RTS than surgery (10.5 vs 23.1 weeks for pubic-related) • Hip dysplasia PAO: greater than 70% RTS competitive athletes, 80%+ with combined arthroscopy1-21

Critical Decision Points for Athletic Hip Pain

  • Not All Imaging Findings Are Pathologic: Cam morphology and labral tears common in pain-free athletes—clinical correlation essential STRONG EVIDENCE
  • Conservative Treatment First: Active rehabilitation (hip/core strengthening) effective for groin pain and many labral tears—trial 6-12 weeks before surgery
  • FAI Surgery Works But Isn't Perfect: 74% return to preinjury level, 12% don't return (persistent pain most common), cartilage damage worsens prognosis
  • Groin Pain Rehabilitation Often Superior: Faster return (10.5 vs 23.1 weeks pubic-related), comparable RTS rates, allows competition during treatment
  • Hip Dysplasia Doesn't End Careers: PAO + arthroscopy achieves greater than 70-80% RTS in competitive athletes, even high-impact sports
  • Timing Matters for Surgery: Early cartilage degeneration, older age, severe dysplasia negatively impact outcomes—don't delay when indicated

Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI) Syndrome

Quick Answer

FAI = abnormal contact between femoral head/neck and acetabular rim causing pain, restricted ROM, and labral/cartilage damage. Three types: CAM (femoral sided—common in athletes), PINCER (acetabular sided), or MIXED. Surgery allows 74% return to preinjury competition level, 87% return to any sport. 12% don't return (persistent hip pain main reason). Conservative management with PT effective for some. Cam morphology present in 37-55% of asymptomatic athletes—imaging findings not equal pathology.1-4

What Is FAI?

Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI) is a dynamic condition where abnormal contact between the proximal femur and acetabulum causes mechanical conflict during hip motion, leading to labral tears, cartilage damage, and hip pain. Two primary morphologic variants exist:

  • CAM-type FAI: Aspherical femoral head-neck junction creates "bump" that jams into acetabulum during flexion/internal rotation. Most common in young male athletes (prevalence 37-55% in asymptomatic athletes). Associated with repetitive hip loading during skeletal development (soccer, hockey, football).
  • PINCER-type FAI: Acetabular overcoverage (excessive depth or retroversion) causes rim impingement. Less common in isolation, more frequent in middle-aged women.
  • MIXED-type FAI: Combination cam + pincer. Most common presentation clinically (86% of surgical cases have mixed morphology).

Critical Concept - Morphology Not Equal Symptoms: Cam morphology present in 37-55% of asymptomatic male athletes and 10-20% of asymptomatic females. Pincer morphology also common in pain-free individuals. Diagnosis requires symptomatic presentation + imaging findings + mechanical impingement on exam—not just imaging alone.2

Development of Cam Morphology in Athletes: Cam deformities develop during adolescent growth in athletes with high hip loading (soccer, hockey, basketball). Repetitive hip flexion + rotation during open growth plates causes femoral head-neck remodeling. Sports with highest cam prevalence: soccer 55%, ice hockey 43%, basketball 37%. Non-athletes: 10-15% prevalence.3

Diagnosis of FAI Syndrome

Clinical Presentation

Typical patient: young adult athlete (15-40 years) with activity-related anterior hip/groin pain. Pain with prolonged sitting (sign of impingement), difficulty with deep hip flexion (squatting, getting in/out of car), mechanical symptoms (clicking, catching, giving way). C-sign positive: patient cups hand over anterolateral hip when describing pain location.

Physical Examination

  • FADIR test (Flexion-Adduction-Internal Rotation): Most sensitive impingement test. Patient supine, examiner flexes hip 90 degrees, then adducts and internally rotates. Positive: anterior hip pain reproduction (sensitivity 78-95%, specificity 10-43%).
  • FABER test (Flexion-Abduction-External Rotation): Hip flexed, abducted, externally rotated into figure-4 position. Positive: anterior pain (less specific for FAI, also positive with labral tears, SI joint, hip flexor pathology).
  • Impingement provocation test: Passive hip flexion greater than 90 degrees + internal rotation. Positive: anterior pain.
  • ROM assessment: Reduced internal rotation in flexion (less than 20 degrees suggests cam impingement). Compare bilateral—asymmetry significant.

Imaging

X-rays (first-line): AP pelvis + lateral hip views. Assess for cam morphology (alpha angle greater than 55 degrees on lateral), pincer morphology (lateral center-edge angle greater than 40 degrees, crossover sign), joint space (narrowing suggests advanced osteoarthritis—poor surgical candidate), Tönnis grade (cartilage degeneration classification 0-3).

MRI (with or without contrast): Visualizes labral tears (present in 90% of FAI syndrome), cartilage defects, bone marrow edema, muscle pathology. MR arthrography increases sensitivity for labral pathology but standard MRI often adequate.

Diagnostic Criteria for FAI Syndrome (Warwick Agreement 2016):

  1. Symptoms: motion or position-related hip/groin pain
  2. Clinical signs: positive impingement test + ROM restriction
  3. Imaging: cam or pincer morphology on x-ray/MRI
  4. Exclusion: significant osteoarthritis (Tönnis grade 2+), dysplasia, other hip pathology

All 4 criteria must be met. Imaging alone insufficient for diagnosis.4

Conservative Management of FAI

Indications: Mild-moderate symptoms, no/minimal cartilage damage, patient preference to avoid surgery, athletes mid-season wanting to delay intervention.

Evidence: 2021 systematic review (n=659) showed 65% pain reduction with active rehabilitation at 6 weeks, with improvements maintained at 12 months. Success higher in cam-type FAI, younger patients, less cartilage damage. No RCTs comparing conservative vs surgery directly.5

Rehabilitation Protocol:

  • Phase 1 (Weeks 0-4): Activity modification (avoid deep flexion, internal rotation), pain control, gentle ROM within pain-free range, core stabilization initiation
  • Phase 2 (Weeks 4-8): Progressive strengthening emphasizing hip abductors (gluteus medius), hip extensors (gluteus maximus), core. Avoid impingement positions. Address kinetic chain deficits.
  • Phase 3 (Weeks 8-12): Sport-specific training, progressive return to activity, movement pattern training (reduce impingement mechanics), plyometrics if appropriate
  • Phase 4 (Weeks 12+): Return to sport if pain-free, full strength, normal movement patterns

Success Predictors: Younger age (less than 30), less cartilage damage (Tönnis 0-1), cam-type FAI, shorter symptom duration (less than 1 year), high compliance with exercises.

Failure Indicators: Persistent symptoms after 12 weeks structured PT, mechanical symptoms (locking, catching), significant cartilage damage on MRI, functional limitations affecting sport/work.

Surgical Management: Hip Arthroscopy for FAI

Indications

  • Failed conservative management (12+ weeks structured PT)
  • Mechanical symptoms (locking, catching from labral tears)
  • High-level athletes with in-season constraints
  • Moderate-severe symptoms limiting activities of daily living/sport
  • Patient preference for definitive treatment

Contraindications

  • Advanced osteoarthritis (Tönnis grade 3, joint space less than 2mm)—consider total hip arthroplasty instead
  • Severe dysplasia (lateral center-edge angle less than 18-20 degrees)—requires PAO, not arthroscopy
  • Active infection, significant medical comorbidities precluding surgery

Surgical Technique

Arthroscopic approach (standard): cam resection (femoral osteochondroplasty), acetabular rim trimming if pincer present, labral repair (preferred) or debridement, loose body removal, capsular management (repair vs release depending on laxity). Surgery duration 60-120 minutes depending on complexity.

Return-to-Sport Outcomes: 2024 Meta-Analysis

Data from 1,634 athletes across 18 studies:

  • Return to same preinjury competition level: 74% (95% CI 67-81%)
  • Return to any sport level: 87% (95% CI 81-92%)
  • Failure to return to sport: 12.1% (95% CI 8.9-15.4%)
  • Reasons for failure to RTS:
    • 74.3% hip-related (persistent pain most common, also stiffness, weakness)
    • 15.2% other medical reasons
    • 10.5% non-medical (retirement, career change, fear)
  • Time to RTS: mean 7.9 months (range 6-12 months)
  • Patient satisfaction: 85-90% satisfied/very satisfied

Heterogeneity high (I² = 83%) due to variable definitions of "return to sport" across studies. Athletes returning to preinjury level represents gold standard outcome.1

Favorable Factors Unfavorable Factors

No/minimal cartilage damage (Outerbridge 0-1) Moderate-severe cartilage damage (Outerbridge 3-4)
Labral repair (vs debridement/resection) Labral resection/debridement only

Complications

  • Overall complication rate: 1.5-7.5% (most studies report 3-5%)
  • Nerve injury: Most common (1-3%). Lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (anterior thigh numbness), pudendal nerve (perineal numbness). Usually transient, resolves 3-6 months.
  • Heterotopic ossification: 0.5-6% depending on prophylaxis use. Indomethacin 75mg daily x 3 weeks reduces risk.
  • Iatrogenic cartilage damage: Less than 1% with experienced surgeons
  • Infection: Less than 0.5%
  • Revision surgery rate: 5-10% within 5 years (persistent pain, inadequate decompression, progression of arthritis)

Athletes vs Non-Athletes: Comparable Outcomes

2023 meta-analysis comparing athletes (n=1,892) vs non-athletes (n=2,148) after hip arthroscopy for FAI found no significant difference in patient-reported outcomes (PROs) or revision rates. Both groups showed large effect size improvements in HHS (mean +25-30 points), iHOT-33 scores. Athletes had slightly higher reoperation rates (7.8% vs 5.9%, p=0.03) but similar satisfaction. Conclusion: high-level athletic participation does not negatively impact surgical outcomes—athletes benefit equally from FAI correction.6

Adolescent Athletes and FAI

Hip arthroscopy in adolescents (ages 12-18) increasingly common, particularly for FAI + labral tears. 2023 systematic review showed:

  • Good-excellent outcomes: 82-95% in adolescent cohorts
  • RTS rates: 85-92% to preinjury level
  • Complication rates similar to adults (3-5%)
  • Concern: Cam morphology may still be developing—premature resection could alter hip biomechanics. Reserve surgery for: failed conservative management, mechanical symptoms, cartilage damage progression on serial imaging.
  • Growth plate status matters: Open triradiate cartilage (younger than 12-14 typically) relative contraindication—risk of growth disturbance. Tanner stage 4-5 (near skeletal maturity) safer.7

Hip Muscle Strength Deficits in FAI

2022 meta-analysis (n=485 FAI patients) found significant hip strength deficits compared to controls:

  • Hip abduction strength: 22% weaker (gluteus medius primary target)
  • Hip extension strength: 18% weaker (gluteus maximus)
  • Hip external rotation: 15% weaker
  • Hip flexion: 12% weaker

These deficits persist even after "successful" arthroscopy if not addressed with targeted strengthening. Rehabilitation must prioritize hip abductor/extensor strengthening to restore normal biomechanics and reduce compensatory patterns contributing to pain.8

Key Takeaway: FAI Surgery

Hip arthroscopy for FAI allows 74% of athletes to return to preinjury competition level, 87% to any sport. 12% fail to return, mostly from persistent hip pain. Younger age, minimal cartilage damage, labral repair (vs resection), shorter symptom duration predict better outcomes. Conservative management effective for many—trial 12 weeks structured PT before considering surgery. Athletes and non-athletes have comparable surgical outcomes.

Hip Labral Tears in Athletes

Quick Answer

Acetabular labrum = fibrocartilaginous rim deepening hip socket, provides stability and shock absorption. Tears common in athletes (33-54% of asymptomatic athletes have labral tears on imaging). Symptomatic tears cause groin pain, clicking, locking. Conservative management: 55% return to sport (can compete during treatment, 27 days lost). Surgical repair: 79% return to sport (324 days lost, higher rate but longer recovery). Labral reconstruction (for irreparable tears) achieves similar outcomes to repair. 2025 consensus: minimum 6 months conservative trial before surgery unless mechanical symptoms severe.9-13

Prevalence: The Asymptomatic Labral Tear Problem

2019 Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis:

  • Asymptomatic athletes: Labral tear prevalence 54% per person, 33% per hip (moderate evidence)
  • Symptomatic athletes: Labral tear prevalence 20% per hip (moderate evidence)
  • Key finding: Labral tears VERY common in pain-free athletes—imaging alone insufficient for diagnosis

Heerey JJ, Kemp JL, Mosler AB, et al. What is the Prevalence of Hip Intra-Articular Pathologies and Osteoarthritis in Active Athletes with Hip and Groin Pain Compared with Those Without? A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. Sports Med. 2019 Jul;49(7):1125-1138. PubMed ID: 30972659.9

  • Conservative management: Lower RTS rate (55%) BUT much faster return (27 days), most can compete during treatment
  • Surgical management: Higher RTS rate (79%) BUT 324 days lost (nearly 11 months)

  • Individualize decision: Season timing, severity of symptoms, mechanical symptoms, patient goals

Labral Repair vs Labral Reconstruction

When labral tissue is irreparable (severe tear, insufficient tissue less than 2mm width, calcified, revision surgery), labral reconstruction uses graft (allograft or autograft) to rebuild labrum.

2024 Meta-Analysis (10 comparative studies, 582 reconstruction, 631 repair):

  • Both effective: Significant improvement in patient-reported outcomes (PROs) at short-term follow-up
  • Repair trended better: Higher scores (not statistically significant), lower risk of conversion to total hip arthroplasty
  • Reconstruction still good: Comparable outcomes when repair not feasible
  • RTS after reconstruction: 81.4% (73.3-90.0%) in athletes—similar to repair11,12

2025 Canadian Consensus Statement on Labral Tears

Modified Delphi process—40 sports medicine physicians (50% orthopaedic surgeons):

  • 76% of statements reached unanimous/strong consensus—high agreement between non-operative and operative physicians
  • Unanimous consensus: RTP timing after surgery, prognostic factors (age, pain severity, dysplasia, degenerative changes), sport-specific training timing after non-operative management
  • Diagnostic imaging: Standing AP pelvis + 45 degree Dunn view MINIMUM for all suspected labral tears
  • Diagnostic injection: Recommended if uncertainty that pain is intra-articular
  • Timeline for re-investigation: Minimum 6 months after surgical treatment before investigating persistent symptoms13

Elite Athletes: Outcomes and RTS

2021 Systematic Review (elite athletes = international/professional level, 22 studies, 1,006 patients, 1,146 hips):

  • Mean RTS rate: 89.9% (range 72-100% across studies)
  • Mean time to RTS: 7.6 months (range 4-12 months)
  • Significant PRO improvement: Modified Harris Hip Score, HOS-ADL, HOS-SSS all improved
  • Sport-specific considerations: Ice hockey players 76-94% RTS, soccer/football 85-95% RTS14

Key Takeaway: Labral Tears

Labral tears extremely common in asymptomatic athletes (33-54% per hip)—imaging findings alone don't equal pathology. Conservative management: 55% RTS, 27 days lost, can compete during treatment. Surgical repair: 79% RTS but 324 days lost. Decision depends on timing, symptoms, season. Elite athletes achieve 89.9% RTS after surgery averaging 7.6 months.

Athletic Groin Pain

Quick Answer

Athletic groin pain = complex, multifactorial—adductor-related, iliopsoas-related, inguinal-related, pubic-related most common. Rehabilitation FIRST: 65% pain reduction with active hip/core strengthening at 6 weeks. Rehab faster RTS than surgery for pubic-related pain (10.5 vs 23.1 weeks). Surgery comparable RTS rates to rehab across diagnostic groups. Risk factors: previous groin injury (strongest), reduced eccentric hip adduction strength, limited hip rotation, inadequate preseason conditioning. 2015 Doha Agreement classification system standardizes terminology.15-19

Classification: Doha Agreement (2015 Consensus)

Athletic groin pain traditionally plagued by inconsistent terminology. 2015 international consensus (Doha Agreement) established anatomy-based classification:

Category Definition Clinical Features
Adductor-Related Groin Pain Pain in adductor region with palpable adductor tenderness Pain with resisted adduction, stretching adductors
Iliopsoas-Related Groin Pain Pain in inguinal region with iliopsoas involvement Pain with resisted hip flexion, stretching hip flexors
Inguinal-Related Groin Pain Pain in inguinal canal region (sports hernia/athletic pubalgia) Pain with Valsalva, cough, palpation of inguinal canal
Pubic-Related Groin Pain Pain localized to pubic symphysis (osteitis pubis) Palpable pubic tenderness, pain with pelvic compression
Hip-Related Groin Pain Hip joint pathology (FAI, labral tear, OA) Positive FADIR, reduced ROM, C-sign

Weir A, Brukner P, Delahunt E, et al. Doha agreement meeting on terminology and definitions in groin pain in athletes. Br J Sports Med. 2015 Jun;49(12):768-74. PubMed ID: 26031643.19

Note: Multiple entities often coexist—not mutually exclusive.

Conservative Management: The Evidence

Active Rehabilitation

2025 Narrative Review findings:

  • Active rehabilitation (hip/core strengthening): 65% reduction in pain (VAS) within 6 weeks vs 40% conventional therapy
  • Manual therapy + exercise: 50% pain reduction at 4 weeks vs 30% exercise alone
  • Most effective: Progressive strengthening (hip adductors, abductors, core), functional exercises, sport-specific training17

Clinical takeaway:

  • Rehab should be first-line for most athletic groin pain
  • Pubic-related pain: Rehabilitation faster return AND comparable success rate
  • Surgery reserved for: Failed conservative trial (6-12 weeks minimum), severe structural pathology

Evidence-Based Rehabilitation Protocol

Hölmich Protocol (RCT—considered gold standard for adductor-related groin pain):

  • Phase 1 (Weeks 1-4): Pain management, gentle stretching, isometric strengthening (adductors, abductors, abdominals)
  • Phase 2 (Weeks 4-8): Progressive resistance training, dynamic strengthening, balance training, sport-specific movements initiated
  • Phase 3 (Weeks 8-12+): Advanced strengthening, plyometrics, gradual return to sport

Copenhagen Adduction Exercise: Single most effective exercise for preventing and treating adductor-related groin pain. Performed 2-3x per week. Side-lying position, top leg supported on bench/partner's ankle, bottom leg lifts toward top leg against resistance. Progressive overload: 3 sets x 8-12 reps, increasing support height as strength improves.

Surgical Options (When Conservative Fails)

Indications: Failed 6-12 weeks structured rehabilitation, persistent symptoms limiting sport/work, structural pathology requiring surgical correction.

Procedures by entity:

  • Inguinal-related (athletic pubalgia/sports hernia): Inguinal floor repair, mesh reinforcement. RTS 8-12 weeks.
  • Adductor-related: Adductor tenotomy/release. RTS 12-16 weeks.
  • Pubic-related (osteitis pubis): Pubic symphysis curettage, bone marrow stimulation. RTS 16-24 weeks.
  • Iliopsoas-related: Iliopsoas release/lengthening. RTS 10-14 weeks.

Outcomes: Surgery achieves comparable RTS rates to rehabilitation but takes longer (see table above). Reserve for true failures of conservative management.

Key Takeaway: Athletic Groin Pain

Rehabilitation is first-line treatment: 65% pain reduction with active hip/core strengthening at 6 weeks. For pubic-related pain, rehab FASTER than surgery (10.5 vs 23.1 weeks) with comparable RTS rates. Previous groin injury and reduced eccentric hip adduction strength are strongest risk factors. Copenhagen adduction exercise most effective for prevention and treatment. Surgery reserved for failed conservative trials (6-12 weeks minimum).

Hip Dysplasia in Athletes

Quick Answer

Hip dysplasia = shallow acetabulum (reduced femoral head coverage) causing overload of labrum/cartilage rim, instability, eventual arthritis. Defined by lateral center-edge angle (LCEA) less than 20 degrees on X-ray. Athletes: periacetabular osteotomy (PAO) corrects bony deficiency—greater than 70% RTS competitive athletes. Combined PAO + arthroscopy (addresses intra-articular pathology): greater than 80% RTS. Don't confuse with FAI: dysplasia = undercoverage, FAI = overcoverage or bony bumps. PAO major surgery but allows high-impact sport participation post-op.20,21

What Is Hip Dysplasia?

Hip dysplasia = developmental condition where acetabulum too shallow, providing insufficient coverage of femoral head.

  • Radiographic definition: Lateral center-edge angle (LCEA) less than 20 degrees (normal 25-40 degrees)
  • Pathophysiology: Reduced coverage → rim overload → labral tears, cartilage damage → pain, instability → early arthritis
  • Opposite of FAI pincer: Dysplasia = undercoverage (too little bone), pincer = overcoverage (too much bone)

Diagnosis

Clinical Features

  • Anterior hip/groin pain (similar to FAI)
  • Instability symptoms: Giving way, apprehension, difficulty with lateral movements
  • Clicking, catching (labral tears common)
  • Positive apprehension test: Abduction + external rotation → sense of instability

Imaging

  • Standing AP pelvis radiograph: Measure LCEA (less than 20 degrees = dysplasia)
  • Tönnis angle: Greater than 10 degrees suggests dysplasia
  • MRI: Assess labral tears, cartilage damage (very common in dysplasia)
  • Borderline dysplasia: LCEA 20-25 degrees—controversial treatment zone

Periacetabular Osteotomy (PAO): The Definitive Treatment

PAO = Bernese periacetabular osteotomy—reorients acetabulum to increase femoral head coverage without disrupting hip joint.

Return-to-Sport After PAO

2023 Systematic Review (6 studies, 341 athletes):

  • Overall RTS rate: Greater than 70% for competitive athletes
  • RTS to ALL sport levels: Low-, moderate-, and high-impact sports participation observed
  • Patient-reported outcomes: Significant improvement (mHHS 81-95, HOOS 72-93)
  • Follow-up: Mean 29.3-100 months
  • Conclusion: PAO efficacious in active patients with severe dysplasia20

Combined PAO + Hip Arthroscopy

Hip dysplasia often accompanied by intra-articular pathology (labral tears, cartilage damage, cam deformity). Combining PAO with hip arthroscopy addresses both bony and soft tissue issues.

2024 Systematic Review (14 studies, 1,083 hips):

  • Mean follow-up: 3.7 years
  • Favorable PROMs: Significant improvement in HOS-ADL, HOS-SSS, mHHS
  • Comparable to PAO alone: No significant difference in outcomes when comparing combined vs isolated PAO
  • Arthroscopy addresses: Labral tears, cam deformity, cartilage pathology at same setting21

2022 Study (29 athletes, minimum 2-year follow-up):

  • RTS rate: Greater than 80% for athletes undergoing combined PAO + arthroscopy
  • Mean follow-up: 34.1 months
  • Significant PRO improvement: mHHS, NAHS, HOS-SSS all improved
  • Minimal clinically important difference achieved: High percentage of patients21

PAO Harms and Benefits

2022 Systematic Review (29 cohort studies):

Benefits (clear and consistent):

  • Pain reduction
  • Improved function (HHS, WOMAC, iHOT)
  • Radiographic correction of dysplasia
  • Greater than 70% RTS for athletes
  • Delays/prevents progression to total hip arthroplasty in many

Harms (real but acceptable given benefits):

  • Major complications: 6-37% (neurologic injury most common—sciatic/femoral nerve palsy, usually transient)
  • Revision surgery: 2-29% (wide range, higher in severe dysplasia)
  • Non-union: 0-7%
  • Conversion to total hip arthroplasty: 4-18% at 5-10 year follow-up
  • Recovery prolonged: Non-weight-bearing 6-8 weeks, full RTS 12-18 months

Prognostic factors for success:

  • Favorable: Younger age (less than 35), minimal cartilage damage (Tönnis 0-1), LCEA 15-25 degrees (vs less than 15 severe), absence of hip OA
  • Unfavorable: Advanced cartilage degeneration, older age (greater than 40), severe dysplasia (LCEA less than 10), pre-existing arthritis

Borderline Dysplasia: PAO vs Arthroscopy Alone

2025 Systematic Review (borderline dysplasia LCEA 20-25 degrees):

  • Both PAO and arthroscopy alone produce favorable outcomes
  • No clear superiority of one approach over other in borderline cases
  • Decision factors: Patient age, symptoms, activity level, presence of cam morphology, cartilage status
  • Arthroscopy advantages: Less invasive, faster recovery, addresses concurrent FAI
  • PAO advantages: Corrects underlying bony deficiency, may prevent long-term arthritis

PAO Recovery Timeline and Expectations

Typical timeline:

  • Hospital stay: 3-5 days
  • Non-weight-bearing: 6-8 weeks (crutches/walker)
  • Partial weight-bearing: Weeks 8-12
  • Full weight-bearing: Week 12
  • Return to low-impact activities: 4-6 months (swimming, cycling)
  • Return to high-impact sports: 12-18 months (running, jumping, contact sports)
  • Peak improvement: 18-24 months post-op

Rehabilitation phases:

  • Phase 1 (0-8 weeks): Protected weight-bearing, ROM exercises, core/upper body strengthening
  • Phase 2 (8-16 weeks): Progressive weight-bearing, gait training, hip strengthening initiation
  • Phase 3 (4-9 months): Advanced strengthening, functional training, sport-specific movements
  • Phase 4 (9-18 months): Return to sport progression, competitive training, performance optimization

Red Flags - When NOT to Perform PAO

  • Advanced osteoarthritis: Tönnis grade 3, joint space less than 2mm—consider total hip arthroplasty instead
  • Severe cartilage damage: Full-thickness chondral defects, extensive delamination—poor prognosis
  • Older age with moderate dysplasia: Greater than 50 years—consider if symptomatic and active, but discuss realistic expectations
  • Normal or overcoverage: LCEA greater than 25 degrees—not dysplasia, consider FAI instead

Key Takeaway: Hip Dysplasia

PAO allows greater than 70% RTS for competitive athletes with hip dysplasia, including high-impact sports. Combined PAO + arthroscopy achieves greater than 80% RTS when intra-articular pathology present. Major surgery with 6-37% complication rate and prolonged recovery (12-18 months RTS), but benefits clear for appropriate candidates. Younger age, minimal cartilage damage, moderate dysplasia (LCEA 15-25) predict best outcomes. Borderline dysplasia (LCEA 20-25) can be treated with either PAO or arthroscopy alone—both favorable.

Disclaimer: Educational content for athletes, coaches, and healthcare professionals. Not a substitute for individualized medical evaluation and treatment. Hip pain requires proper diagnosis—FAI, labral tears, groin pain, and hip dysplasia have overlapping symptoms but different treatments. Imaging findings (cam morphology present in 37-55% of asymptomatic athletes, labral tears in 33-54% of pain-free hips) common in asymptomatic athletes—clinical correlation essential. Conservative management should be attempted for 6-12 weeks before surgery in most cases except severe mechanical symptoms. FAI surgery: 74% return to preinjury level, 12% fail to return (persistent pain main reason). Labral tears: conservative 55% RTS (27 days lost, can compete during treatment) vs surgical 79% RTS (324 days lost). Groin pain: rehabilitation first-line (65% pain reduction at 6 weeks, faster RTS than surgery for pubic-related). Hip dysplasia: PAO allows greater than 70% RTS competitive athletes but major surgery with prolonged recovery (12-18 months). Always consult qualified healthcare providers (sports medicine physician, orthopedic hip surgeon, physical therapist with hip expertise) for diagnosis, treatment planning, rehabilitation progression, and return-to-sport clearance.

Risk Acknowledgment: FAI surgery complications 1.5-7.5% (nerve injury 1-3% usually transient, heterotopic ossification 0.5-6%, revision surgery 5-10% within 5 years). Cartilage damage (Outerbridge 3-4), older age (greater than 40), labral resection vs repair, long symptom duration (greater than 5 years) negatively impact outcomes. Labral tear surgery: 324 days lost vs 27 days conservative, though surgical RTS rate higher (79% vs 55%). Elite athletes achieve 89.9% RTS but mean 7.6 months recovery. Groin pain surgery comparable RTS to rehabilitation but slower return (23.1 vs 10.5 weeks for pubic-related). Previous groin injury strongest risk factor for recurrence—address residual deficits. PAO major surgery: 6-37% major complications (neurologic injury most common, usually transient), 2-29% revision surgery, 4-18% conversion to total hip arthroplasty at 5-10 years. Non-weight-bearing 6-8 weeks, RTS 12-18 months. Advanced cartilage degeneration (Tönnis grade 2-3), older age (greater than 40), severe dysplasia (LCEA less than 10) predict worse PAO outcomes. Borderline dysplasia (LCEA 20-25) can be treated with either PAO or arthroscopy—both favorable but different recovery profiles.
